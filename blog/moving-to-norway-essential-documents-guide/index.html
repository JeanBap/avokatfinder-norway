Essential Documents for Moving to Norway: The Complete Expat Guide (2026)

Introduction: Your Roadmap to Norwegian Integration (600 words)

Moving to Norway is an exciting opportunity—whether you're relocating for work, love, adventure, or a fresh start. The Scandinavian nation consistently ranks among the world's best places to live, boasting excellent infrastructure, generous social benefits, stunning natural landscapes, and a high quality of life. However, the administrative process of integrating into Norwegian society can feel overwhelming, especially if you're arriving from outside the European Economic Area or dealing with complex documentation requirements.

The Norwegian government has streamlined much of its immigration and registration process in recent years, but the system remains rigorous and requires careful attention to detail. Unlike some countries where you can muddle through with minimal paperwork, Norway maintains meticulous records and treats administrative compliance seriously. Your success in establishing yourself here depends largely on having the right documents in the right order before you arrive—or at least knowing exactly what you'll need once you're on Norwegian soil.

This guide will walk you through every essential document and registration step required to move to Norway. We'll cover the apostilles and translations you need from home, the intricacies of the Norwegian residency registration system (Folkeregisteret), how to obtain your fødselsnummer (national identity number), the specific requirements for non-EEA citizens through the Directorate of Immigration (UDI), and how to set up BankID—Norway's ubiquitous digital authentication system that you'll use dozens of times daily.

We'll also address the practical documentation needs for banking, healthcare, property rental, and tax registration. Each section includes specific timelines, government office contacts, and the precise documents required. By the end, you'll have a comprehensive checklist and understand the sequential order in which to tackle each step.

One critical note: Norwegian bureaucracy moves methodically. Timelines are rarely fast, and immigration authorities will request additional documents just when you think you're done. This is normal and doesn't indicate a problem with your application. The good news is that once you've jumped through these hoops, Norway's digital systems and social infrastructure reward your patience with exceptional efficiency.

Many expats find the documentation process challenging enough that they hire specialist immigration lawyers to handle it. If you're planning to stay long-term, own property, establish a business, or simply want to ensure every step is handled perfectly, this is worth considering. The cost is minimal compared to the peace of mind, and it frees you to focus on other aspects of your move.

Before You Leave Home: Apostilles, Translations & Home Documents (1,200 words)

The most common mistake expats make is arriving in Norway with documents that aren't properly certified or apostilled. Norwegian authorities will reject unverified documents, forcing you to request new copies from your home country—a process that can take weeks or months from abroad. Prevent this by handling all document preparation before you leave.

Understanding the Apostille Requirement

Norway is a signatory to the Hague Apostille Convention, which means all official documents from other countries must carry an apostille—a specific certification stamp that confirms the document's authenticity. This isn't a Norwegian requirement alone; it's an international standard that every country party to the convention uses when accepting documents from abroad.

Your apostille must be obtained from the authority that issued the original document. For a birth certificate issued by your city or county, you'll apply to that municipal office. For educational credentials, contact your university or school's registrar. For employment verification, your employer or HR department can provide this. For marriage certificates and other vital records, contact the vital statistics office in your jurisdiction.

The process varies by country and institution, but generally takes 1-4 weeks. Start this process immediately—don't wait until you're ready to move. Request multiple copies of each document (at least 3-4) so you have backups and can provide copies to different Norwegian authorities without waiting for more to arrive.

Which Documents Require Apostilles?

Any official document you'll submit to Norwegian authorities needs an apostille. This includes:

  • Birth certificate – Essential for all residents, required for Folkeregisteret registration, banking, and healthcare
  • Marriage/divorce certificates – Needed if you're married, divorced, or separated; required for social benefits and housing applications
  • Educational credentials – Bachelor's, master's, or professional diplomas if you're seeking employment based on qualifications
  • Employment contracts & verification – If you have a job offer from a Norwegian employer, they may request apostilled verification from your previous employer
  • Police clearance certificate – Required for residence permits; most countries issue this from a national police or justice ministry
  • Divorce decrees – If applicable; courts issue these
  • Medical records – For specialized healthcare; your doctor or hospital issues these
  • Professional licenses – If you hold professional certifications (medical, legal, engineering, etc.), get apostilles from the issuing body

Translation Requirements

Norway requires that all documents not in English, Norwegian, Swedish, or Danish be officially translated by a certified translator. Official translation means certified by a translator recognized by the Norwegian courts or by an official translation service in your home country. Machine translations, even from professional translation apps, are not accepted.

Many apostille services or the issuing authority can recommend certified translators. In the EU, the Court of Justice websites often list certified translators. Start gathering these translations before you leave home—translation turnaround times can be surprisingly long, especially if the translator specializes in less common language pairs.

Each translated document must also be signed by the translator and include a statement that it's an accurate translation of the original document. The translator may also need to include their credentials or registration number.

Residency Registration in Norway: Step-by-Step (1,800 words)

The centerpiece of Norwegian immigration is the Folkeregisteret (the Population Register), managed by the Norwegian Tax Administration (Skatteetaten). Registration here is mandatory if you'll be living in Norway for more than six months. The process has multiple stages, and the timeline varies depending on your citizenship and circumstances.

Stage 1: Arrival and Initial Registration

When you arrive in Norway, you should register with the Folkeregisteret within two weeks of arrival. You can do this either through your municipality's administrative office (Fylkesmannen) or through some employers' HR departments if they handle immigration. This registration is free and establishes your residence address in Norway's official system.

Bring your passport, proof of accommodation (lease agreement, purchase contract, or letter from your host), proof of employment (job contract) or proof of funds (bank statements showing sufficient resources to support yourself), and completed registration form DUF 1. If your documents are in a non-English language, bring certified translations. This is your first touchpoint, and it triggers the machinery of your legal residency status.

After initial registration, you'll receive written confirmation (usually within a few days). This confirmation letter is crucial—keep multiple copies, as you'll need it for banking, healthcare registration, and housing contracts.

Stage 2: The D-Number (Temporary ID)

Within days of your initial Folkeregisteret registration, you should receive a D-number, a temporary identification number used for non-residents. The D-number is an 11-digit code (similar format to a fødselsnummer) that's your identification while your permanent status is processed. You'll need this for employment, opening bank accounts, and renting property.

The D-number is typically assigned automatically and sent to your registered address. If you don't receive it within a week, contact your local tax office. Some employers and banks can look up your D-number if you can confirm your identity and date of birth.

Stage 3: The Fødselsnummer (Permanent National ID)

The fødselsnummer is your permanent 11-digit national identity number, derived from your birthdate, personal number, and checksum. For non-Norwegians, obtaining this takes significantly longer than the D-number—typically 6 to 12 weeks. However, you can function in Norwegian society with your D-number until the fødselsnummer arrives.

The fødselsnummer is assigned based on your residence status. If you're an EEA citizen, you'll receive it automatically once your residency is registered and confirmed. If you're a non-EEA citizen, you must be approved for a residence permit first (see below), and then the fødselsnummer follows. Once assigned, the Tax Administration (Skatteetaten) sends it to your registered address in an official letter.

Critical: Do not estimate your fødselsnummer or ask someone to guess it based on your birthdate. Norwegian authorities verify every number against their database, and errors will delay all subsequent processes. Wait for your official letter from Skatteetaten confirming your number.

Non-EEA Citizens: UDI Residence Permits

If you're not a citizen of an EEA country or Switzerland, you must obtain a residence permit from the Directorate of Immigration (Utlendingsdirektoratet, or UDI) before you can receive a permanent fødselsnummer. The main categories are:

Work permit: Requires a concrete job offer from a Norwegian employer who's verified that no EEA workers could fill the position. Your employer must sponsor your application, which typically takes 6-12 weeks. You must have sufficient income to be self-supporting.

Family reunification: If you're joining a spouse, partner, or family member already in Norway, you need documentation of your relationship, their residence, and financial support proof. Processing time is typically 3-6 months.

Student visa: Requires acceptance to a Norwegian educational institution and proof of sufficient funds. Valid for the duration of study plus a grace period.

Self-employment: If you're establishing a business, you need a detailed business plan, proof of startup capital, and documentation that the business will provide sufficient income. Processing time is typically 8-12 weeks.

Your employer, immigration consultant, or a specialist lawyer can help with UDI applications. All supporting documents must be apostilled and translated. UDI applications are submitted through the UDI website (udi.no) and require uploading all documents in PDF format.

Once your residence permit is approved, UDI sends you a residence permit card (borettskort) or an approval letter. You can then register this with the Folkeregisteret, and your permanent fødselsnummer assignment will follow.

What You'll Need for Folkeregisteret Registration

  • Valid passport
  • Proof of residence (lease, purchase contract, or invitation letter)
  • Employment contract or proof of funds (bank statements, student scholarship letter)
  • Completed form DUF 1 (for initial registration) or DUF 2 (for permanent registration after permit approval)
  • Birth certificate (apostilled and translated)
  • Marriage/divorce certificates (if applicable, apostilled and translated)
  • Police clearance certificate (for some residence permits, apostilled and translated)

The Folkeregisteret registration is managed at the municipality level. Visit your local Fylkesmannen office or contact them by phone or their website to book an appointment. In larger cities like Oslo, Bergen, and Stavanger, you can often book appointments online. Processing times vary: initial registration is usually same-day or next-day, while formal residence confirmation takes 2-4 weeks.

BankID: Norway's Digital Identity System (600 words)

BankID is not optional in Norway—it's the standard for digital authentication used by virtually every government agency, bank, insurance company, employer, and major business. If you want to access government services, manage your tax return, sign documents, or even complete many job applications, you need BankID.

BankID is a digital signature and identification system operated by a consortium of Norwegian banks. It's integrated into your bank account and accessed through your bank's online platform or a mobile app. To set up BankID, you need a Norwegian bank account first (see Banking section below).

Once you have a bank account, BankID is typically enabled automatically, and you'll receive instructions to activate it through your bank's security procedures. The setup requires confirming your identity, setting a security code, and often completing a video call with your bank to verify you're the legitimate account holder.

BankID is required to: sign your tax return, access your healthcare records, apply for government housing assistance, sign rental contracts, file permit applications, and access government portals like the NAV (Norwegian Labor and Welfare Service) website. Without it, you'll find yourself unable to complete essential tasks. This is another reason why many expats hire specialist lawyers—they already have BankID and can handle digital submissions on your behalf until you're set up.

Tax & Fiscal ID in Norway (1,000 words)

Norway's tax system is progressive and, for residents, fairly straightforward. Upon registering in the Folkeregisteret, the Tax Administration (Skatteetaten) automatically opens a tax account using your fødselsnummer or D-number. You don't need to apply for a separate tax ID; your national identification number serves this purpose.

Tax Residency Status

You become tax-resident in Norway if you live there for more than six months in a calendar year, regardless of your citizenship. Once tax-resident, you must file an annual tax return (selvangivelse) for income earned worldwide. Norway taxes global income for residents, but provides tax credits for taxes paid to other countries to avoid double taxation.

Your employer automatically deducts tax from your salary (through the skattecard system), so if you're employed, most of your tax obligations are handled via withholding. Your employer sends your tax information to Skatteteaten automatically, and you'll receive a pre-filled tax return in March each year.

Filing Your First Tax Return

In March, Skatteetaten sends your pre-filled tax return (selvangivelse) to your address or makes it available through their online portal (which requires BankID to access). Review the return carefully—it should include all salary income reported by your employer. Verify that deductions and withholdings are correct. Most expats simply approve the pre-filled return; if you have self-employment income or foreign income sources, you'll need to add these.

The deadline for filing is May 17 (which coincidentally is Norwegian Constitution Day). If you're filing as an expat for the first time, contact Skatteetaten directly—they have English-speaking staff and can guide you through the process. Filing electronically via their portal (skatteetaten.no) is free and straightforward once you have BankID.

VAT for Business Owners

If you're establishing a business in Norway, you'll need to register for VAT (merverdiavgift) once your business revenue exceeds 50,000 NOK annually. Registration is through Brønnøysund Register Centre (Brønnøysundregistrene) and can be completed online. VAT returns are filed quarterly or monthly depending on your volume.

Deductions and Credits Available to Expats

As a resident, you may be eligible for several deductions: home office expenses (if self-employed), travel expenses for commuting (partial), care expenses for children or elderly relatives, medical expenses (limited), and contributions to private pensions. Keep receipts for all significant expenses and maintain detailed records.

Banking in Norway (800 words)

You need a Norwegian bank account almost immediately. Without one, you can't get paid (most employers require a Norwegian account), you can't pay rent or utilities, and you can't establish credit history. Opening an account is straightforward but requires in-person verification at a branch.

Choosing a Bank

The major Norwegian banks are DNB, Nordea, Handelsbanken, and Santander. Smaller banks like Sparebank1 and regional banks also exist. Most offer free checking accounts for employees and expats. DNB is the largest and has the most extensive branch network, making it a practical choice for first-time account holders.

Opening Your Account

Visit your chosen bank's branch with your passport, proof of address (tenancy agreement or landlord letter), and proof of income (employment contract). They'll verify your identity, usually through a brief video call with a bank representative if you're opening online. The account is typically activated within 24-48 hours, and they'll issue you a card and PIN.

Some banks require a minimum deposit (typically 100-500 NOK) to open an account. Ask about this when you visit. Once opened, the account comes with online banking access and the option to enable BankID.

BankID Setup

As mentioned earlier, BankID is enabled through your bank account. After your account is opened, you'll receive instructions (usually via email or SMS) to activate BankID through their online portal. You'll need to set a security code and confirm your identity, sometimes through a video call. This process typically takes 1-3 days.

International Transfers & Currency Exchange

Norway uses the Norwegian Krone (NOK). If you need to transfer money from your home country, use international bank transfers (SWIFT transfers). Your Norwegian bank can provide their SWIFT code. Be aware that international transfers can take 3-5 business days and may incur fees from both your home bank and your Norwegian bank.

For regular transfers of large amounts, consider using specialist money transfer services (like Wise, OFX, or Remitly), which often offer better exchange rates than banks. If you're married to a Norwegian or have family there, domestic transfers are free and instantaneous.

Healthcare Registration (800 words)

Norway has a public healthcare system (Helsestasjon) that provides coverage to all residents. Once registered in the Folkeregisteret, you're automatically enrolled in the public system and assigned a GP (huselege). Healthcare is primarily financed through taxes, and most care is free or requires only a small co-payment.

Registration Process

Upon registration, the municipality automatically assigns you a GP. You'll receive a letter with your GP's name and clinic location. Your GP is your first point of contact for all non-emergency care and referrals to specialists.

Getting Your Health Insurance Card

You don't need a separate health insurance card in Norway—your fødselsnummer or D-number functions as your health ID. When visiting a doctor or hospital, provide your identity number, and they'll pull up your records.

Healthcare for Non-Residents and Short-Term Visitors

If you're in Norway temporarily (less than 6 months), you're not covered by the public healthcare system. Purchase private travel health insurance before arriving. EU/EEA citizens can use their European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) for emergency care.

Dental and Prescription Medications

Public healthcare in Norway does not cover routine dental care (only emergency treatment). You'll need to purchase private dental insurance or pay out-of-pocket. Prescription medications are subsidized through the Norwegian medicines subsidy system; once you've paid a certain amount out-of-pocket annually (approximately 2,300 NOK), medications become free for the rest of the year.

Property & Rental Documentation (1,000 words)

Whether you're renting or buying, Norwegian property transactions involve significant documentation and legal processes.

Renting an Apartment or House

In Norway, residential leases are typically 12-month contracts governed by the Housing Leasehold Act (Husleieloven). Landlords must register the lease with the municipality, and tenants are strongly protected. A lease typically requires: your passport, proof of income (employment contract or salary slip), proof of financial stability (bank statements), and references from previous landlords or employers. Some landlords request a background check, which you can arrange through a rental agency or your lawyer.

Expect to pay a security deposit (typically one month's rent) and in some cases a deposit for utilities. The lease must specify the monthly rent, deposit amount, start date, utilities included/excluded, and termination terms. Never sign a lease without understanding these terms.

Buying Property

Buying property in Norway is complex and involves: a property inspection, legal review of the deed, negotiation of purchase terms, mortgage arrangement, and formal registration with the Land Registry (Tinglysingskontoret). Non-citizens have some restrictions: you cannot buy land for holiday homes or investment without special permission, and you must be resident or planning to reside in the property. Working with a real estate lawyer is essential; they typically charge 2,000-4,000 NOK for the transaction.

The purchase process involves an offer (tilbud) which, once accepted by the seller, creates a binding contract. You have several days to complete inspections and hire a lawyer. The actual transfer is registered with the Land Registry after payment clears. The entire process typically takes 4-8 weeks.

Essential Property Documents

  • Lease agreement (Leiekontakt): Must be in writing and registered with municipality
  • Property deed (Tinglysingsdokumenter): Official property ownership documentation from the Land Registry
  • Building certificate (Byggesertifikat): Confirms the building complies with building codes
  • Energy label (Energimerking): Required for all buildings; shows energy efficiency
  • Inspection report (Tilstandsrapport): Property condition assessment
  • Mortgage agreement (Pantedokumenter): If you're financing the purchase

Bringing It All Together: Your Action Plan (600 words)

Move to Norway with this sequence in mind:

6 months before departure: Request apostilles and start translation process. Book appointments with your country's authorities.

2 months before: Secure housing through websites like Finn.no or Airbnb (if temporary). Apply for UDI permits if required. Contact your employer about employment contract details and coordinate arrival date.

1 month before: Register with your local Norwegian municipality or embassy to understand local requirements. Arrange medical records transfer if needed. Organize travel and accommodation for first days.

Week of arrival: Register with Folkeregisteret. Start bank account process immediately. Register with healthcare system.

Days 1-2 after arrival: Settle into accommodation. Register with municipality's tax office. Contact local police for any required registrations.

Week 1: Open bank account and enable BankID. Contact your employer about tax card setup (skattkort). Register with local healthcare provider.

Weeks 2-4: Receive D-number. Monitor mail for fødselsnummer. Set up utility accounts (electricity, internet, water). Register address with insurance companies.

Weeks 4-12: Receive fødselsnummer. Update all official accounts with permanent number. Complete BankID setup. File preliminary tax return if needed.

Throughout: Save all receipts and documentation. Maintain backup copies of all documents. Keep contact information for your lawyer, bank, tax office, and healthcare provider accessible.
