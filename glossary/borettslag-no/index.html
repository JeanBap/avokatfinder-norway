Borettslag (Housing Co-operative)

A borettslag is a Norwegian housing co-operative where residents own shares (andeler) rather than their individual units. It is one of the two main ownership structures for flats in Norway, the other being eierseksjonssameie.

The borettslag model is deeply embedded in Norwegian housing history, originally developed by OBOS and USBL as a way to provide affordable housing after World War II. Today borettslag units account for a substantial proportion of the Oslo apartment market. Understanding the model is essential for anyone buying a Norwegian flat.

In a borettslag, the co-operative owns the land and buildings. Residents own an andel — a share that gives them the exclusive right to occupy a specific unit. This right is called borett. When you 'buy' a borettslag flat, you are technically buying an andel and its associated borett, not the bricks and mortar themselves.

Each borettslag is governed by its own vedtekter (statutes) and run by an elected styret (board). The styret manages maintenance, finances, and compliance. Major decisions — like taking on new fellesgjeld for a renovation — require a vote of the general assembly (generalforsamling).

Ownership transfer must be approved by the co-operative. In practice, approval is virtually automatic for standard residential purposes, but the right of first refusal (forkjopsrett) held by the co-operative's founding housing association (e.g. OBOS members) can delay completion by up to 20 days while members exercise their option to buy at the agreed price.

Borettslag units can only be owned by individuals or certain approved entities — limited companies generally cannot own andeler. This restricts institutional investors and keeps borettslag housing predominantly owner-occupied.

Common Mistake

Watch out: OBOS forkjopsrett can catch buyers off guard. If the property is in an OBOS borettslag, an OBOS member can jump the queue and buy at your agreed price within 20 days of the deal becoming public. Your offer may be trumped.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Check whether the borettslag has a forkjopsrett agreement with OBOS or another housing association before submitting your bid. Your estate agent should know — if they don't, check the borettslag's own vedtekter.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is forkjopsrett and how does it affect me as a buyer?

It is a pre-emption right held by members of a housing association (commonly OBOS). They can buy the property at the price you agreed within 20 days. If exercised, your purchase does not proceed and your deposit is refunded.

Can I rent out a borettslag flat?

Short-term rentals and sub-letting require the styret's approval. Extended or commercial sub-letting is often restricted or prohibited. Check the vedtekter before buying if rental income is part of your plan.

Is a borettslag unit harder to mortgage than a freehold flat?

Banks lend on borettslag andeler routinely in Norway. They assess totalpris (unit price + fellesgjeld share). The process is standard but the fellesgjeld component affects your overall borrowing capacity.
