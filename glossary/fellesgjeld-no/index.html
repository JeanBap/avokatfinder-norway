Fellesgjeld (Shared Debt in Housing Co-operatives)

Fellesgjeld is the collective mortgage debt of a Norwegian borettslag (housing co-operative) that every member implicitly takes on when buying a unit. It is added to the unit price to calculate the true purchase cost.

Norwegian housing co-operatives (borettslag) own the underlying property collectively and typically carry a shared mortgage (fellesgjeld) taken out to finance original construction or major renovation. When you buy a unit in a borettslag, you are buying an andel (share) in the co-operative, not freehold title. Part of your monthly fee (felleskostnader) services your proportional share of the fellesgjeld.

The critical number for buyers is the totalpris: the advertised purchase price plus the unit's share of fellesgjeld. A flat listed at NOK 3.5 million with NOK 1.2 million fellesgjeld has a true acquisition cost of NOK 4.7 million. Banks assess mortgage affordability based on totalpris, not the asking price.

Fellesgjeld interest is deductible in Norwegian personal income tax, but only proportionally through your membership in the co-operative — the co-operative files the deduction on your behalf and reports it to the tax authority (Skatteetaten).

Some borettslag offer innskuddslaan — an arrangement where members can pre-pay their share of the fellesgjeld, reducing monthly costs. This can be attractive if you have capital available, but check whether the co-operative allows it and what the prepayment terms are.

Before buying into a borettslag, request the latest annual report (arsberetning) and budget. High fellesgjeld relative to the unit value, combined with upcoming maintenance projects, can signal that monthly fees are about to rise sharply.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Comparing two borettslag flats on asking price alone is misleading. Always compare totalpris and the monthly felleskostnader, which reflects both fellesgjeld interest and running costs.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Ask for the borettslag's five-year maintenance plan (vedlikeholdsplan) before bidding. A roof or lift replacement scheduled for next year could mean a special levy (ekstrainnskudd) on top of your regular fees.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is fellesgjeld the same as my personal mortgage?

No. Fellesgjeld is the co-operative's debt, not yours personally. Your personal mortgage from your bank is separate and in addition to the fellesgjeld component.

Can I lose my apartment if the co-operative defaults on fellesgjeld?

In theory yes, but it is extremely rare. Norwegian borettslag are tightly regulated and lenders require adequate reserves. Check the co-operative's financial health before buying.

What is the difference between a borettslag and an eierseksjonssameie?

An eierseksjonssameie involves freehold ownership of individual sections (seksjoner) with no fellesgjeld concept. In an eierseksjonssameie you own your unit outright and take your own mortgage directly.
