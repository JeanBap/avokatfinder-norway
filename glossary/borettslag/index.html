Borettslag

Borettslag Norway – Housing Cooperative Ownership Explained

A borettslag (housing cooperative) is a cooperative housing company that owns a building or group of buildings, while its members hold andeler (shares) entitling them to the exclusive use of specific dwelling units. Buying a flat in a Norwegian borettslag means purchasing the andel — not the property itself. This is the most common apartment ownership form in Norway.

Each borettslag has a collective debt (fellesgjeld) — loans taken by the cooperative for building construction, renovation, or maintenance. This debt is shared between all members. Buyers must understand that the total cost of ownership includes not just the purchase price of the andel but also the buyer's share of the fellesgjeld. Failing to account for fellesgjeld is the most common financial mistake in Norwegian apartment purchases.

The monthly fee paid by members (fellesutgifter or husleie) covers building maintenance, common utilities, property insurance, and interest and repayment of the fellesgjeld. A borettslag with high fellesgjeld will typically charge higher monthly fees; future interest rate rises will also increase these fees.

Key Facts

Ownership unitAndel (cooperative share)
Collective debtFellesgjeld — add to purchase price for true cost
Monthly feeFellesutgifter / husleie
Pre-emption rightForkjøpsrett — 20 days for other members
ManagementStyret + generalforsamling

The borettslag is managed by a board (styret) elected at the annual general meeting (generalforsamling). Major decisions require member approval; routine management is handled by the board or a professional property manager (forretningsfører, often OBOS or similar). The board's accounts and meeting minutes are essential due diligence documents.

Andeler can only be sold with the cooperative's consent in certain circumstances, and the borettslag has a right of first refusal (forkjøpsrett) under Norwegian law. The forkjøpsrett allows any member to purchase the andel at the agreed price within a statutory period — typically 20 days — after the sale is agreed.

⚠ Common MistakeComparing borettslag prices without accounting for fellesgjeld. An andel priced at NOK 2 million with NOK 1.5 million fellesgjeld costs more in total than one priced at NOK 3 million with no fellesgjeld.
💡 Expert TipAlways calculate the total cost (prisantydning + fellesgjeld + acquisition costs) and the effective monthly burden (fellesutgifter + imputed mortgage cost on your borrowing). Many Norwegian property portals (Finn.no) now display totalpris (total price) to facilitate this comparison.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a bank lend against a borettslag andel?

Yes. Banks lend against borettslag andeler in the same way as for ordinary property, though the fellesgjeld is factored into the overall debt assessment.

What is OBOS?

OBOS is Norway's largest housing cooperative organisation, managing thousands of borettslag in Oslo and nationally. Many Norwegians are OBOS members, which gives them forkjøpsrett in OBOS-managed cooperatives.

Is a borettslag the same as a sameie?

No. A sameie (co-ownership) is a structure where flat owners hold proportional title to the building directly, without a cooperative intermediary. Sameier tend to have lower collective debt.
