Avhendingsloven

Avhendingsloven is Norway's Sale of Property Act governing the sale of real property between private parties, establishing the legal framework for disclosure obligations, buyer inspection duties, and remedies for defects.

The Avhendingsloven (Act on Sale of Property, LOV-1992-07-03-93) is the primary legislation governing private property sales in Norway. It establishes the rights and obligations of buyers and sellers, including what information must be disclosed, what constitutes a defect, and what remedies are available when the property does not conform to expectations.

The Act was significantly amended in 2022 to strengthen buyer protection. The most important change was effectively ending the as-is clause (selges som den er) as a blanket exclusion of seller liability. Sellers must now provide detailed condition reports (tilstandsrapport) prepared by an authorised building inspector, and as-is clauses only limit liability within the scope of properly disclosed matters.

A property has a defect (mangel) under the Act if it does not conform to what the buyer was entitled to expect, whether due to physical condition, legal status, or misleading information. Remedies include price reduction, repair, rescission of the contract, and damages, depending on the severity and nature of the defect.

The buyer also has an inspection duty (undersøkingsplikt). After being given the opportunity to inspect, the buyer cannot rely on defects they discovered or should have discovered during reasonable inspection. The interplay between seller disclosure and buyer inspection duty is the source of most post-sale property disputes in Norway.

Because the Act imposes significant disclosure obligations on sellers, Norwegian practice involves detailed tilstandsrapporter (building condition reports) produced by RICS-equivalent building surveyors before listing. Buyers should read these carefully and commission additional specialist surveys (e.g. radon, rot) when the report flags concerns.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Avhendingsloven apply to commercial property sales?

No. Commercial property is governed by general contract law and the specific terms of the purchase agreement. The Act applies only to residential and holiday property sales.

How long do I have to claim defects after purchase?

The general limitation period is 5 years from the sale date. However, you must notify the seller of a defect within a reasonable time after discovering it (reklamasjon). Delayed notification can forfeit your right to remedies.

Can the seller exclude liability for specific known defects?

Yes, if the defect is fully and accurately disclosed in the condition report and sale documentation, the seller can limit liability for it. Disclosure must be clear and not buried in technical language.
