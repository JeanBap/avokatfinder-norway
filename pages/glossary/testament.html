Testament

Definition: A Norwegian will that specifies how a person's estate should be distributed after death.

Overview

A testament in Norwegian law is a formal document in which a person declares how their property should be distributed upon death. It must be created while the testator has full legal capacity and testamentary intent.

Norwegian law recognizes several forms of testament: holographic wills (handwritten), wills witnessed by two witnesses, and notarized wills. Each form has specific requirements regarding signature, dating, and witness attestation.

A valid testament must clearly identify the testator, detail the distribution of assets, and be signed and dated. Wills that fail to follow proper procedures may be challenged or declared invalid by courts.

In Norway, a testament can designate an executor (testamentseksekutor) to manage the estate and ensure the testator's wishes are carried out. It can also include specific bequests, charitable donations, and conditions on inheritances.

Norwegian law imposes mandatory share rules (pliktdeler), meaning certain close relatives (children, spouse) have a right to at least half the share they would receive under intestacy, even if the will attempts to exclude them.

Key Facts

  • Formal document declaring asset distribution after death
  • Must be dated and signed by testator
  • Can be handwritten, witnessed, or notarized
  • Can designate executor and include specific bequests
  • Subject to mandatory share (pliktdeler) rules

Common Mistake

Watch out: Creating an informal testament without proper witnesses, or failing to account for mandatory share rules that protect close relatives.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is required for a valid Norwegian testament?

The will must be dated, signed by the testator, and either handwritten or witnessed by two independent adults.

Can I completely exclude my children from my testament?

No. Norwegian law guarantees children a mandatory share (pliktdeler) of at least half their statutory inheritance.

Who can be an executor in Norway?

Any competent adult can serve as executor, though courts typically appoint a family member, lawyer, or bank unless the will designates otherwise.
